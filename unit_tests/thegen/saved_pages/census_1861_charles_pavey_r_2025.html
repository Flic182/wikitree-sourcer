Pre-1841 Census

"Census" were taken in 1801 to 1831 however, these were statistical in nature and did not contain a list of names. Some areas did collect names as part of this however, those only exist for a very small number of locations.

How the census forms are organised:

England and Wales

The General Register Office was responsible for taking the census, so it used the registration districts already in place for the registration of births, marriages and deaths. These were then divided into smaller enumeration districts (EDs) which enumerators were recruited for.

Scotland

Until 1855 Scotland did not have a law requiring registration of births, marriages and deaths. Therefore for 1841/1851 they used the civil parish as the district. In 1855, Scotland was divided into 1028 registration districts (RD). While they were based closely on the civil parish, some had to be divided and others united to form the new districts. Each of these were given a unique reference number (often with a suffix to sub-divide larger areas).

Enumeration districts usually covered about 200 families in towns and an overall journey of 15 miles for the enumerator in the country. They included smaller institutions, boats and barges on inland waterways, tents, caravans and barns.

Larger public institutions such as barracks, prisons and hospitals as well as hotels and inns had their own special schedules. The head of the institution was the enumerator. In 1861 this applied to establishments with 50 or more persons usually resident. In 1871 the number had increased to 135 and in 1881 to 200. From 1891 to 1911 it was revised again to upwards of 100.

How the census was taken:

In every census year an enumerator delivered a form to each household in the country for them to complete. The heads of household were instructed to give details of everyone who slept in that dwelling on census night, which was always a Sunday. The forms completed by each household, known as schedules, were collected a few days later by the enumerator.

From 1841 to 1901 the information from the schedules was then copied into enumeration books. Once the enumeration books had been completed, most household schedules were then destroyed.

In 1911 all the household schedules were kept, for the first time, and were not copied into enumeration books.

1841

The census was taken on Sunday, 6 June 1841 and was the first to list the names of every individual, which makes it the earliest useful census for family historians. However, less information was collected than in later census years.

Questions

  • Forename and Surname
  • Age (rounded down to the nearest five years for those aged 15 or over, although this instruction wasn't always followed)
  • Sex
  • Occupation
  • Whether they were born in the county where they were enumerated (Y or N)
    • Ireland (I)
    • Foreign Parts (P)
    • Scotland (E) (England and Wales Census)
    • England (E) (Scotland Census)

Notably, questions were not asked about marital status and head of household (and thus relationship to the head of household).

1851 and 1861

The census was taken on Sunday, 30 March 1851 and Sunday, 7 April 1861

Questions

  • Forename, middle names (often just initials) and Surname
  • Relationship to the head of the household
  • Marital status
  • Age (at last birthday)
  • Sex
  • Rank, profession or occupation
  • Where born – county and parish if born in England or Wales, country only if born outside England and Wales)
  • Whether blind, or deaf and dumb
  • Scotland Only:
    • Number of children aged between five and 15 attending school or being educated at home
    • Number of rooms with one or more windows

1871 and 1881

The census was taken on Sunday, 2 April 1871 and Sunday, 3 April 1881

Questions

  • As 1851 and 1861, except for the following difference:
  • The last column now reads: 1. Blind 2. Deaf and Dumb 3. Imbecile or Idiot 4. Lunatic
  • 1871 Scotland Only:
    • "Unemployed” could be added to the occupation field if people in a particular trade or profession were out of work.
    • The question on the number of children attending school or being educated at home was amended to between five and 13 years
  • 1881 Scotland Only:
    • Count of Gaelic speakers

1891

The census was taken on Sunday, 5 April 1891..

Questions

  • As 1871 and 1881 with the following extra details on employment:
  • Whether Employer, Employed, or Neither Employer nor Employed
  • Scotland Only:
    • Count of Gaelic speakers changed to whether a person spoke Gaelic (G) or Gaelic and English (G & E)
  • Wales Only:
    • language spoken

1901

The census was taken on Sunday, 31 March 1901..

Questions

  • As 1891, with occupation details changed to:
  • ‘Employer, Worker or Own account’
  • A new column ‘If working at home’
  • Scotland Only:
    • Gaelic question restricted to those aged three years and over
    • Persons born abroad were asked to indicate if they were “foreign subjects”
  • Isle of Man Only:
    • language spoken

1911

The census was taken on Sunday, 2 April 1911..

Questions

  • For married women only, the number of years of their present marriage, the number of children born of that marriage, the number still living, and the number that had died.
  • As well as their occupation, the industry in which the person was employed. If employed by a government, municipal or other public body, the name of that body.
  • Parish and county of birth for anyone born in the UK (which included all of Ireland). If born elsewhere in the British Empire, the colony or dependency, and the state or province.
  • For anyone born outside England and Wales, whether they were resident or visitor in the country.
  • Nationality of anyone born overseas whether British by parentage, British by naturalisation (including year of naturalisation) or, if a foreign national, of which country.
  • In the Infirmity column, the age at which the person had become afflicted.
