    Early church and district codes

    You might notice a code on the documents you find when researching family ancestry. Here you can match the code to the church or district where the birth, marriage or death was recorded.

    On this page
    • Search early church codes
    • Search district codes

    Search early church codes

    The early church records cover the years 1787 to 1855 and are a collection of baptism, marriage and burial records held by churches in colonial New South Wales.

    Find the relevant church denomination as well as parish, circuit or district using the two letter code on your documentation.

    In 1856 the registry acquired the Supreme Court's copies of the 1834 to 1835 baptism, marriage and burial registers from ministers of denominations other than Church of England.

    In 1879 the Clergy Returns Transfer Act was passed, requiring that the the Church of England Bishop in Sydney hand over copies of the 1825 to 1855 baptism, marriage and burial registers to the Registry.

    Mr Kerrison James who held the records was paid 4000 pounds compensation for the loss of the records. When opened, the crates containing the registers were found to also contain earlier registers going back to 1787.

    In 1912 the Registrar General wrote to all church authorities asking for access to their pre-1856 registers so that registry and church records could be reconciled. The registrar also asked for access to the 1856 to 1895 church marriages registers.

    The registry's early church records were consolidated into 158 volumes of approximately 50,000 entries, with each given a unique number or early church code.

    Some early marriage records recorded only the details of the two people marrying with no details of the parents even though they appeared in the Church registers. These registrations were amended with a note explaining the change.

    The registry's records from the early days of NSW are not complete and the reconciliation of records and amendments to marriage registrations was never finalised. Records still held by the churches can sometimes fill in the missing details on a marriage certificate or provide baptism records where there is no civil registration for the birth.
